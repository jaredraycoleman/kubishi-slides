# Tei-hiimü — Our family

Family terms in OVP almost always appear with a **possessive prefix** — you rarely say "a mother" in the abstract, you say "my mother", "your father", "his sister".

Most family terms have two forms:

- **Unpossessed** — ends in `-bi` (e.g. `piabi` "a mother")
- **Possessed** — drops `-bi` and takes a prefix (e.g. `i-bia` "my mother")

Possessive prefixes (from Lesson 6): `i-` my, `ü-` your, `a-/ma-` his/her (near), `u-` his/her (far), `ta-` your & my, `tü-` his/her own.

---

# Parents

| Unpossessed | "my ___" | English |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| `piabi` | `i-bia` | mother |
| `nawabi` | `i-nawa` | father |

The `p → b` change is a **fortis/lenis softening** that happens after a prefix.

---

piabi | a mother
i-bia | my mother
ü-bia | your mother
a-bia | his/her mother (nearby)
u-bia | his/her mother (far)
nawabi | a father
i-nawa | my father
ü-nawa | your father
a-nawa | his/her father (nearby)
u-nawa | his/her father (far)

---

# Siblings

OVP distinguishes **older** vs. **younger** siblings:

| Unpossessed | "my ___" | English |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| `pabi'ibi` | `i-babi'` | elder brother |
| `hamma'abi` | `i-hamma'` | elder sister |
| `wanga'abi` | `i-gwanga'` | younger brother |
| `pünni'ibi` | `i-bünni'` | younger sister |

Consonant changes after the prefix: `p → b`, `w → gw`.

---

i-babi' | my elder brother
i-hamma' | my elder sister
i-gwanga' | my younger brother
i-bünni' | my younger sister
ü-babi' | your elder brother
ü-hamma' | your elder sister
ü-gwanga' | your younger brother
ü-bünni' | your younger sister
a-babi' | his/her elder brother (nearby)
a-bünni' | his/her younger sister (nearby)

---

# Cross-gender sibling

`saaŵu` is special: a man uses it for his **sister** (or female cousin), a woman uses it for her **brother** (or male cousin). The term flips depending on who's speaking.

---

saaŵu | sister (man speaking) / brother (woman speaking)
püa' | same-gender sibling or cousin

# Grandparents

OVP distinguishes **maternal** (mother's side) and **paternal** (father's side):

| OVP | English |
|-----|---------|
| `mu'a` | maternal grandmother (mother's mother) |
| `togo'` | maternal grandfather (mother's father) |
| `hutsi'` | paternal grandmother (father's mother) |
| `künu'` | paternal grandfather (father's father) |
| `tsoo'` | great-grandparent |

These terms work both directions — `mu'a` also means "maternal grandchild" *spoken by the grandmother*.

---

mu'a | maternal grandmother (mother's mother)
togo' | maternal grandfather (mother's father)
hutsi' | paternal grandmother (father's mother)
künu' | paternal grandfather (father's father)
tsoo' | great-grandparent
i-mu'a | my maternal grandmother
i-dogo' | my maternal grandfather
i-hutsi' | my paternal grandmother
i-günu' | my paternal grandfather

---

# Aunts and uncles

Also split by side of family:

| OVP | English |
|-----|---------|
| `pidu'` | maternal aunt (mom's sister) |
| `puu'` | maternal uncle (mom's brother) |
| `pahwa` | paternal aunt (dad's sister) |
| `nazaguŵa` | paternal uncle (dad's brother) |
| `nawatsi'` | uncle (general) |

Fun fact: `tabuu'tsi'` (cottontail rabbit) literally means "our little uncle" — `ta-` (our) + `puu'` (uncle) + `-tsi'` (little).

---

pidu' | maternal aunt (mom's sister)
puu' | maternal uncle (mom's brother)
pahwa | paternal aunt (dad's sister)
nazaguŵa | paternal uncle (dad's brother)
nawatsi' | uncle (general)
i-bidu' | my maternal aunt
i-buu' | my maternal uncle
i-bahwa | my paternal aunt

# Spouses

| Unpossessed | "my ___" | English |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| `kuŵabi` | `i-guŵa` | husband |
| `nodügwa` | `i-nodügwa` | wife |

`k → g`, `w → gw`.

Related: `nananodügwa` = married couple, `kuŵadu` = to marry (said of a woman, "to make a husband"), `nodügwadu` = to marry (said of a man, "to make a wife").

---

kuŵabi | a husband
i-guŵa | my husband
ü-guŵa | your husband
nodügwa | a wife
i-nodügwa | my wife
ü-nodügwa | your wife
nananodügwa | married couple

# Children and young people

| OVP | English |
|-----|---------|
| `ohaa'` | baby, infant |
| `bibi'` | baby (from English) |
| `naatsi'` | boy |
| `tsüa'` | girl |
| `wobokasa` / `woboka` | baby boy |
| `wasüüya'` | baby girl |
| `süadümmü` | young woman |

The `-tsi' / -chi'` suffix means "little" and is very productive — you can add it to almost any noun for affection or smallness, and even stack it (`woda'-tsi'-chi'` = "a little tiny chipmunk").

---

ohaa' | baby
bibi' | baby
naatsi' | boy
tsüa' | girl
wobokasa | baby boy
wasüüya' | baby girl
süadümmü | young woman

# Family in sentences

Family terms behave like any noun — they take subject suffixes (`-ii` near / `-uu` far) and object suffixes (`-neika` / `-noka`) from earlier lessons.

---

I-bia-ii tüka-ti. | My mother (nearby) is eating.
Ü-nawa-uu mia-ku. | Your father (far) went.
A-babi'-ii hibi-ti. | His elder brother (nearby) is drinking.
A-gwanga'-uu katü-ti. | His younger brother (far) is sitting.
I-dua-ii mia-wei. | My son (nearby) will go.
Ü-bedü-uu tüka-ku. | Your daughter (far) ate.
Nüü ü-bia-neika a-buni-ti. | I see your mother.
Mahu i-nawa-neika a-buni-ti. | He sees my father.
I-mu'a-ii nüga-ti. | My maternal grandmother is dancing.
I-dogo'-uu üwi-ti. | My maternal grandfather is sleeping.
